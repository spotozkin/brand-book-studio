# Brand Book Structure and Archetype Examples

## Standard section order

Every brand book built with this skill follows this sequence, scaled to the chosen depth tier (see `asset_inventory.md`). Skip a section entirely rather than padding it if it's genuinely not applicable (e.g. a solo creator may not need "brand architecture").

1. **Cover** — brand name/wordmark, one-line essence statement
2. **Brand essence** — purpose, audience, positioning statement
3. **Messaging** — tagline, elevator pitch, message hierarchy, proof points
4. **Voice and tone** — adjectives with sounds-like/doesn't-sound-like pairs, 2-3 short example phrases in-voice
5. **Logo system** — primary lockup, simplified mark, mono/reverse versions, clear space, minimum size, misuse examples
6. **Color system** — palette with hex/RGB, accessible pairings, usage notes (what's primary vs. accent vs. background-only)
7. **Typography** — primary/secondary typefaces, type scale, web/print/presentation usage notes, license source
8. **Imagery and graphic language** — photography/illustration/icon style, any pattern or motif
9. **Applications** — realistic examples: social post, slide, document header, web hero, etc., scaled to depth tier
10. **Accessibility and provenance** — contrast confirmations, font licenses, any AI-assisted asset disclosure
11. **Governance** (Tier 3 only) — review process, versioning, brand architecture
12. **Asset index** — what's included, file formats, last updated

## Archetype notes

These are starting points to adapt, not templates to copy verbatim — always write actual content for the specific brand in front of you.

### Startup / tech

Tends toward a confident, plain-spoken voice with little ornamentation. Palette: one saturated brand accent against a clean neutral grayscale, plus a clearly distinct success/error color for product UI. Typography: a clean grotesk or humanist sans for both display and body, prioritizing legibility in dashboards and docs over personality. Imagery: product screenshots, abstract geometric/spatial graphics, minimal posed photography. Watch for: sounding indistinguishable from every other B2B SaaS brand — push for one or two specific, ownable details (a phrase, a visual motif, a stance) rather than safe genericism.

### Boutique retail / lifestyle

Tends toward an intimate, sensory voice — concrete and specific rather than abstract superlatives. Palette: a muted, low-saturation core with one richer seasonal or signature accent. Typography: a contrast pairing, often an elegant serif for display against a quiet, restrained sans for body. Imagery: tactile close-up photography, natural light, disciplined negative space. Watch for: defaulting to generic "premium lifestyle" aesthetics that could belong to any brand in the category — anchor every visual choice back to something specific about this brand's actual product or story.

### Nonprofit / mission-driven

Tends toward a direct, hopeful, evidence-grounded voice — concrete outcomes over abstract mission language. Palette: trust-signaling colors (often blue/green family) paired with strong, accessible neutrals and a clear action/donate color. Typography: prioritize legibility above all, especially for donor reports and accessibility compliance. Imagery: real program photography over staged stock, charts/maps where they support credibility. Watch for: mission statements that sound interchangeable with every other nonprofit — push for specific proof points and named outcomes rather than aspirational adjectives alone.

### Personal / professional services (e.g. a physician, consultant, or solo practitioner)

Tends toward a voice that balances expertise with approachability — credible but not cold. Palette: often anchored to something personally meaningful (a family color, a practice's existing signage) paired with one or two refined neutrals; restraint reads as more credible than a large palette in this category. Typography: a distinctive display face for warmth (a serif or editorial sans) paired with a highly legible workhorse body face, since this content is often read by people who are anxious, tired, or skimming (patients, clients). Imagery: real photography of the person and their actual work environment over generic stock, when available. Watch for: over-designing past what the actual use cases need — a solo practice usually needs a website, a few printed materials, and consistent social presence far more than an elaborate sub-brand system.
