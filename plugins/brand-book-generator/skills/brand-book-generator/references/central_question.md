# The Central Question — What Is THIS Book Actually Solving?

Run as **Step 2.6**, after the posture is confirmed and before any outline. Takes five
minutes; changes everything downstream.

## Why this step exists

The professional corpus (23 books studied) never shares a skeleton because those books
were never answering the same question. NASA 1976 answers "how do we standardize marks
across thousands of federal applications?" A fashion book answers "how do we keep
restraint from collapsing into blandness?" A collaboration-software book answers "how do
we stay friendly at enterprise scale?" **The document's structure falls out of its
question.** A generator that asks one universal question ("what is this brand's complete
system?") produces one universal skeleton, however well it varies the clothes. This step
replaces the universal question with the brand's own.

## The procedure

1. **Draft the question.** In ONE sentence, state this brand's specific governance
   problem — the thing most likely to go wrong when other people produce material in its
   name. Not the mission, not the positioning: the failure mode this document exists to
   prevent. Good form: "protecting X from Y."
   - A heritage watchmaker: "protecting a century of restraint from marketing enthusiasm."
   - A toy brand on a licensed-IP-heavy site: "keeping the brand's own colors sovereign
     on pages flooded with partner artwork."
   - A three-location clinic: "keeping three front desks from drifting into three brands."
   - A solo creator: "making tomorrow's hire produce work the founder would recognize."

2. **Validate against evidence.** Check the question against the Step 1.5 asset call and
   Step 2 strategy: does the collected material actually show this risk? (Screenshots
   full of partner logos → the sovereignty question is evidenced. No partners anywhere →
   it isn't; re-derive.) **A central question that can't point to evidence is worse than
   no question** — it will organize the book around a fiction. If no single question
   survives validation, fall back to the posture's default section spine and weights;
   that is a legitimate outcome, not a failure.

3. **Confirm it with the person** in one line inside the strategy check-in you're already
   doing at the end of Step 2 — "the way I see it, this document's real job is [question];
   fair?" — not as a separate interview round.

4. **Let it govern three things:**
   - **Page allocation.** The question's territory gets the deep chapters; everything
     else compresses to its floor. In the professional corpus, page allocation is
     diagnostic — you can read what a brand was worried about from where the pages went.
     A brand with no voice problem gets a half-page of voice; a brand whose whole risk is
     co-branding gets three spreads on it.
   - **The opening move.** The book may open ON the problem (page one shows the partner
     logo next to the brand mark and draws the line) rather than on generic essence. The
     strongest professional books start where the pain is.
   - **Which rules get named vs. merely enforced.** A brand that would talk about its own
     methodology gets named principles; a brand that never would gets the same rules
     applied silently. (See `structural_seeds.md`, "naming the doctrine.")

## What this step must NOT do

- It must not drop floor content (the posture's completeness floor in
  `document_postures.md` still binds — the question sets weights, never coverage).
- It must not override the confirmed posture; it operates within it.
- It must not produce a question so generic it would fit any brand ("keeping the brand
  consistent"). If the question would survive a find-and-replace of the brand name with a
  competitor's, it isn't the question yet.

## Record it

Write the confirmed question as an HTML comment at the top of the book's `<body>`
(`<!-- CENTRAL QUESTION: ... -->`) and as a field in the AI Brand Kit. The
anti-fingerprint gate checks that the outline can be justified from it.
