# Asset Inventory and Depth Tiers

A brand book shouldn't be padded to look impressive — it should match what the person actually needs at their current stage. Use whichever tier the intake points to (or ask directly, see `intake_questions.md` Round 4).

**Important: visual execution is not a tier.** Every brand book, even Tier 1, should feel like a real, finished object — a table of contents, an actual rendered mark (see `mark_techniques.md`), color-block section dividers, and a closing spread are baseline expectations at every tier, not upsells. What scales between tiers is *how much content* is documented (how many application examples, whether there's a UI component system, whether governance exists), not *how polished* what's there looks. A three-page Tier 1 book should still look alive; it's just shorter.

## Tier 1: Minimum viable

For brand-new businesses, solo creators, or anyone who needs to start using a consistent brand *this week*.

- Cover, table of contents, and closing spread (the bookends that make it feel like a real document)
- Positioning statement and 3-4 voice/tone adjectives with sounds-like/doesn't-sound-like pairs
- An actual rendered mark (monogram badge or simple motif — see `mark_techniques.md`) plus a logo variation grid showing it on 4-5 backgrounds
- Primary + secondary color palette (3-5 colors total), with hex/RGB and a confirmed accessible text/background pairing
- One primary and one secondary typeface, basic type scale
- One-paragraph imagery direction (not a full style guide)
- 2-3 example applications shown in context (e.g. a social post and a document header)

## Tier 2: Strong launch

For an established business doing its first real brand documentation, or a rebrand that needs to actually roll out across channels.

Everything in Tier 1, plus:

- Full messaging hierarchy: tagline, elevator pitch, 3-4 proof points, sample headlines
- "We are / we are not" and "every touchpoint should make people feel" sections, plus 1-2 say-this/not-this copy example pairs
- Expanded color system: neutrals, one or two semantic/accent colors, contrast-checked pairings for both light and dark use
- Full type scale across web, print, and presentation contexts, with fallback stack
- A pattern system page: the mark repeated as a texture, with usage rules
- A UI components page: buttons, cards, a stats/credibility bar, navigation preview
- A mini "brand in use" mockup — a compact fake webpage combining nav, hero, stats, and cards in one composition
- A fuller application set beyond the two Tier 1 examples
- A short do's-and-don'ts page (common misuse patterns to avoid)

## Tier 3: Mature system

For larger organizations, multi-program nonprofits, or businesses with sub-brands, multiple locations, or a design team that needs governance, not just a starting point.

Everything in Tier 2, plus:

- Brand architecture (how sub-brands, programs, or locations relate to the master brand)
- Motion/animation principles if the brand has digital products
- Data visualization / chart style (if the brand publishes reports or dashboards)
- Localization or multilingual notes if relevant
- A versioning and brand-review governance section (who approves new applications, how updates get communicated)
- Full asset package index: every file, its source, its license, and last-updated date

## Choosing a tier when it's ambiguous

Default down on *content volume*, not on *finish quality*. A short Tier 1 book that's actually polished and complete beats a Tier 3 book that's half generic filler. If someone seems to want more than their stage warrants, it's fine to say so and suggest starting at Tier 1 or 2 with room to expand later.
