# Worked examples — the two modes, illustrated

These are **illustrative composite case studies** — fictional brands constructed to teach patterns proven across real engagements. No real client's identity, palette, or details appear here. They exist so the skill can pattern-match an incoming request to a proven engagement *shape*, not so it can copy these brands.

The single most important lesson: **there are two fundamentally different jobs that both get called "make me a brand book," and they must be diagnosed in Step 1.**

---

## Mode A illustration: "Luma Sleep Coaching" — personal expert brand, built from scratch
*(Archetype: solo expert / coach / personal-professional brand launching with no existing identity.)*

**Brief shape on arrival:** Rich verbal context (credentials, mission, audience) but zero visual assets. Everything — mark, palette, type, voice — is invented in the engagement.

**Strategy decisions that drive everything downstream:**
- Positioning deliberately runs *against* category defaults. Sleep/wellness coaching skews either clinical-medical or crystals-and-lavender soft. Luma chooses warm, evidence-based, and plainspoken. This one contrarian call determines the palette (warm and confident, not spa-pastel), the type (a serif with personality, not a tech grotesque), and the copy rules (no jargon, no mysticism).
- Tagline drafted to make the tone adjectives real, e.g. "Rest, on the record." — science-forward but human.

**Visual system decisions:**
- A larger palette works only with a strict *hierarchy narrative*: one loud primary that "carries the brand everywhere," a darker sibling reserved for interactive states, and an accent color constrained to exactly three named places. Write the hierarchy as prose rules, not just swatches — "the accent appears in the stats bar, the footer, and the mark's crown, and nowhere else" is enforceable; a swatch grid isn't.
- Geometric mark built from 3–4 basic shapes (per `mark_techniques.md`), each layer mapped to a named palette color *with a meaning*. Design the mark so it *is* the pattern system (a repeating strip with a stated minimum count and a banned background) — one asset, two jobs.
- Type: a display serif with a featured italic style + a clean sans. If an italic or alternate style carries a tone adjective, document it as a first-class style, not an afterthought.

**Voice section that works:** "We are / We are not" paired columns, a five-word emotional-target row, and say-this/not-this pairs contrasting human copy against category jargon ("You're not broken — your schedule is." vs "Suboptimal sleep hygiene presents multifactorial etiology."). Copy examples come from the client's actual domain, never generic filler.

**Audience-driven applications:** a coach who sells through talks and a waitlist gets talk-pitch CTAs, a credibility stats bar, program cards, and a segmented contact form — not a generic business-card spread. Build the applications section from the brand's actual conversion goals.

**Family-system note:** if a client mentions a related brand (a sibling practice, a parent company), ask whether the systems should be visual relatives — shared DNA, distinct expression — before designing in isolation.

## Mode B illustration: "Meridian Physical Therapy" — established practice, codified from its existing site
*(Archetype: established business with a real but undocumented identity — professionally designed website, real logo files, zero documentation.)*

**Brief shape on arrival:** Screenshots of the live website + the URL. Explicit instruction: **create nothing new.** The job is extraction and organization — turn an identity that already exists in practice into a reference document for staff, vendors, and anyone doing creative work.

**What Mode B changes about every step:**
- **Step 2 (strategy) becomes archaeology, not invention.** Positioning, differentiators, and proof points are *read off* the existing site and marketing, then organized — not brainstormed. Every claim in the book must already exist somewhere the client has published it. The verified-accuracy standard applies with full force: a fact you can't source from their existing materials doesn't go in the book.
- **Step 3 (visual system) becomes measurement, not design.** Colors are sampled from the live site and locked with an explicit closed-set rule: "If a color isn't on this page, it isn't a brand color." That closed-set sentence is the core value of a codification book — it's what stops brand drift.
- **Handle third-party visual noise explicitly.** Live sites carry colors that aren't the brand's — embedded review-widget stars, social icons, booking-platform buttons. Name what's on the site but NOT part of the brand, or staff will sample it.
- **Fonts you can see but can't name:** if the live site renders a licensed webfont whose exact name is unknown, document the *typographic behavior* (family class, weights, case, tracking), set the book itself in open-license equivalents, state that plainly in an "In practice" note, and route font confirmation to whoever built the original identity. Never guess a font name into a governance document.
- **Never redraw the logo; index the real files.** The book's asset index lists actual filenames (vector master, print files, sized rasters, icon variants) with a use-for column. Where an asset *doesn't exist* (say, no reversed wordmark for dark backgrounds), the book says so and routes the request to the original design firm — it does not fabricate the missing variant. Fabricating a missing logo variant in a codification book is the cardinal sin of Mode B.
- **Credit and route to the original designer.** A codification book documents someone else's design work; it should say whose, and direct new-asset requests to them.
- **Governance is a first-class section, not a footnote.** For a staff audience: a "quick rules" prose block naming the only approved text pairings, a shared-folder structure (logos / brand book / templates, view-only for staff), and operational rules like "never screenshot a logo off the website — always pull from the folder."
- **Accessibility documented with receipts:** measured contrast ratios per approved pairing, and any inherited color that fails AA for text explicitly demoted to decorative-only. In Mode B you inherit the palette; the accessibility section's job is drawing the line around what the inherited colors may and may not do.

**Tone of the document itself:** operational and directive, written to the staff member or vendor ("give these files to printers, never web rasters"). A Mode B book is a rulebook first, an inspiration piece second — almost the inverse of Mode A.

## Diagnosing the mode in Step 1

Ask or infer early: **"Does a brand already exist in the wild that we're documenting, or are we creating one?"**

Signals for Mode B: an established business, a live professionally-designed website, uploaded screenshots of existing materials, language like "organize," "document," "for the staff," "so vendors stay consistent." In Mode B: do not run the visual-preference intake questions, do not invent, extract — and treat any gap (missing logo variant, unknown font) as a documented gap with a routing instruction, never as a design opportunity.

Signals for Mode A: no existing identity, "starting," "launching," "rebrand," visual-preference answers wanted. Full skill workflow applies.

Hybrid exists (existing logo + nothing else): Mode A workflow seeded by the real logo file, per the existing-logo guidance in SKILL.md.

## Revision & QA patterns that recur across engagements

- The most common first-draft fixes are copyfitting (overflow, awkward breaks) and placeholder text surviving to export — the render-to-PNG visual check in Step 5 exists because of this; do not skip it.
- Full-bleed dark cover/divider pages are the highest-impact "finished-feeling" element; build the cover and interior dividers from the brand's own motif.
- Close with a back-cover restatement (motif field + tagline + one-line identity) — cheap to build, disproportionately professional.
